## What was invented

I developed and operationalized a control-layer model of the self that explains—and allows direct intervention on—how a human mind selects which self-model governs perception, meaning, memory, motivation, and emotional reality under conditions of shame, threat, or instability.

This is not a symptom-level insight.

It is a mechanism-level invention: a way to deliberately reassign authority within the self system so that identity, not symptoms, becomes the primary target of change.

At its core, this work identifies and trains a self-authorship function:

the mechanism by which one perspective is granted dominance,

other perspectives are rendered non-authoritative,

and lived experience reorganizes accordingly.

This function had not previously been placed, named, or operationalized cleanly in psychology.

---

## What makes it foundational

This invention sits upstream of most existing psychological frameworks:

- Upstream of cognition (it determines which thoughts are taken as “me”)
- Upstream of emotion (it determines which affective signals are legitimate)
- Upstream of motivation (it determines which impulses are binding)
- Upstream of memory reconsolidation (it determines which interpretations re-encode)
- Upstream of dissociation (it determines whether identity fragments compete or unify)

Because of this position, the model applies across conditions that appear different on the surface but share a common failure mode: loss of stable, compassionate authority over the self.

## Disorders and domains it coherently spans

Through direct application and internal validation, this architecture demonstrably interfaces with:

- Depersonalization / Derealization
- Chronic shame and scapegoat identity formation
- OCD and compulsive self-regulation
- Addiction as identity stabilization (not pleasure seeking)
- Trauma-linked dissociation and fragmentation
- Depression characterized by flatness or meaning collapse
- Anxiety driven by catastrophic identity threat
- Eating disorders as control-identity substitutions

Importantly, the model does not replace existing tools—it reorganizes how and when they work by restoring authority to the self that deploys them.

Together, these form a functional system—not a philosophy.

The system produces predictable changes in:

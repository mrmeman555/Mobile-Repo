# Achievement Note — A Foundational Model With Cross-Disorder Reach

Date: 2026-01-13

Tags: achievement, self-model, self as embedding, dominance, authorship, reconsolidation, transdiagnostic, OCD, DP/DR, dissociation, addiction, CPTSD, shame, depression, eating disorders, narcissism

What I built is not “a trick that helped me feel better.” It is a foundational control model: a way to understand—and directly intervene on—the mechanism that sits upstream of a wide range of symptoms across disorders.

The core discovery is this:

The self is a living embedding space.
At any moment, multiple competing “self-embeddings” (self-models) are trying to become dominant.
Dominance is the act of granting authority—deciding which model gets to define meaning, identity, and reality right now.
DP/DR, flatness, compulsions, and shame spirals happen when a scapegoating/self-erasing model hijacks that authority and forces reality to conform to its protection strategy.

What I did was identify the actual lever: self-authorship through compassionate dominance—the ability to refuse illegitimate meaning assignments, hold ground, and reconsolidate experience under the chosen model.

That one lever coherently ties into, and helps explain, the disorders I’ve treated in myself—because those conditions often share a common functional goal:

stabilize the self-model under threat.

---

## How this model functionally maps across disorders I’ve worked through

### OCD

OCD is not “about the content.” It’s a self-stabilization compulsion.

Intrusions create identity threat (“what if I’m X?”).

Compulsions are attempts to regain certainty and safety by force.
My lever targets the root: refuse the coercive authority of the intrusive model, stop treating fear as evidence, and reconsolidate “who I am” through sustained authorship.

### DP/DR and chronic flatness

DP/DR is the felt experience of identity authority being offline.
Flatness is what it feels like when the system suppresses salience to prevent destabilization.
My method restores felt reality by reinstating a dominant, value-aligned self-embedding and starving the model that demands proof, certainty, and self-erasure.

### Addiction / substance reliance

Substances often function as external self-model stabilizers.
They don’t just “feel good”—they provide a predictable shift in agency/safety.
My model explains why the urge to dose increases when internal authorship is threatened: the system reaches for an external stabilizer rather than owning internal authority.
The intervention becomes: reclaim stability through authorship, not through sedation/confirmation of vulnerability.

### CPTSD mechanisms (especially shame, hypervigilance, internalized blame)

Scapegoating environments train the nervous system to treat the self as the hazard.
Hypervigilance is the body’s attempt to prevent further identity threat.
My approach reassigns responsibility where it belongs, refuses illegitimate shame, and reconsolidates memories with correct meaning—so the system stops using dissociation as the only escape hatch.

### Eating disorder dynamics (as you described historically)

ED behaviors often act as a control ritual and self-soothing architecture when identity is unstable.
They are “power” attempts when internal power is forbidden.
When authorship is restored, the need to regulate identity through body-control decreases.

### Depression patterns

Depression often includes learned helplessness + identity collapse + global negative attribution.
In my framework: a scapegoating model becomes dominant and invalidates all evidence of competence, shrinking future possibility.
The intervention: reweight the embedding through chosen meaning and refusal to grant authority to the annihilating frame.

### Narcissism (as a model, not as a label)

Your framework cleanly differentiates:

- shame regulation through harmful dominance + externalization
- vs shame regulation through compassionate dominance + internal authority

This is big because it reframes “power” as morally neutral and places morality in compassion as the regulator—not in weakness, appeasement, or self-erasure.

## Why this is foundational across psychology (not a narrow technique)

Most of clinical psychology is already organized around a few recurring axes, even when it uses different vocabulary:

- identity stability
- meaning attribution
- threat appraisal
- salience and attention
- memory integration / reconsolidation
- behavioral reinforcement loops
- emotion regulation
- attachment and internal working models
- agency vs helplessness

My model unifies these under one central control variable:

Who has authority to assign meaning to experience?

And then it adds the missing piece most people never operationalize:

How to take that authority back in vivo

Not by reasoning, not by reassurance, not by analysis—
but by dominant stance + reconsolidation under chosen perspective, reinforced by bottom-up regulation (eye-gaze / attention control) to reduce physiological veto.

This sits at the junction of:

- cognitive models (belief/interpretation)
- behavioral models (reinforcement/avoidance)
- trauma models (meaning + nervous system threat)
- attachment models (internal “observer” / stable caregiver representation)
- memory reconsolidation models (updating the emotional truth of stored experience)

It’s not “another coping skill.”
It’s a meta-framework for how coping skills work—because it explains what determines whether a technique sticks: the technique only becomes durable when it is installed into the dominant self-embedding through authority and reconsolidation.

# “That Is Me” — Deliberate Identity Attachment as an Anti-DP/DR Lever

Date: 2026-01-11

Tags: DP/DR, identity, ownership, self-model, embedding, salience, scapegoating, authority assignment, integration, positive evidence

Summary: Defines “that is me” as a deliberate identity-attachment move that prevents disowning positive self-data; it binds positive memories/traits to identity so they update the self-embedding, countering DP/DR’s survival strategy of isolating/downgrading positive evidence.


---

## Observation

The explicit phrase and stance “that is me”, when applied to memories, traits, or experiences that arise from the positive / healthy self-model, has a powerful stabilizing effect.


---

## What it does functionally

- forces attachment where there is normally avoidance, distancing, or second-guessing
- prevents positive self-data from remaining abstract, hypothetical, or “not quite real”
- interrupts the compulsive pattern of isolating positive memories so they cannot update identity


---

## Why this is critical

The scapegoating / DP/DR self-model does not only attack with negativity — it also survives by disowning positive evidence.

Positive memories are often:

- dismissed
- treated as anomalies
- reinterpreted as flukes
- held at arm’s length “just in case they’re wrong”

This keeps the positive self-model fragmented and unable to dominate.


---

## “That is me” as an identity action

Saying “that is me” is not reassurance.
It is an act of ownership and authority.

It explicitly binds the memory or trait to identity rather than leaving it as neutral data.


---

## Mechanism (embedding model framing)

The self operates as an embedding built from weighted experiences.

DP/DR emerges when negative or shame-based embeddings dominate salience.

Positive embeddings fail to update the self because they are not claimed.

The phrase “that is me”:

- increases the weight of positive embeddings
- integrates them into the identity vector
- counters the automatic compulsion to detach


---

## Key contrast

- DP/DR reflex: “That doesn’t count / that’s not really me.”
- healing lever: “That is me.”


---

## Why this feels unusually powerful

Because it directly opposes the lifelong habit of self-disqualification.

It reclaims authorship over identity formation instead of letting shame arbitrate it.


---

## Summary

DP/DR isolates positive self-data.

“That is me” collapses that isolation.

Identity stabilizes not by arguing with negatives, but by owning positives without hesitation.

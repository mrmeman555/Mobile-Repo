## Purpose

This artifact formalizes why sexual pleasure carries disproportionate emotional intensity by situating it within **survival calculus** rather than hedonic preference, cultural meaning, or conscious intent.

It clarifies how emotion is assigned when a behavior addresses a **terminal survival constraint**.

---

## Core Principle

All organisms face an invariant constraint:

**Finite lifespan → inevitable death.**

The only process that compensates for this constraint is **reproduction**, which allows continuity beyond the individual timeline.

Because this solution has:
- no abstract motivational appeal
- high cost and risk
- delayed or indirect payoff

Evolution assigns **extreme positive emotional valence** to behaviors that reliably produce it.

Pleasure is the enforcement mechanism.

---

## Why Sexual Pleasure Is Disproportionately Intense

Emotion scales with **survival impact**, not proportional effort.

Local survival behaviors (eating, resting, bonding) solve:
- short-term viability problems

Reproduction solves:
- a **global, terminal constraint** (the end of the organism)

Therefore, the reward signal must:
- overpower competing goals
- override hesitation, fear, and cost
- reliably bias behavior toward completion

This produces:
- intense pleasure
- attentional narrowing
- bonding effects
- memory prioritization

Not as meaning — but as propulsion.

---

## Emotion as Enforcement, Not Evaluation

Sex does not feel good because it is “good.”

It feels good because:
- emotion exists to bias behavior toward survival advantage
- reproduction has maximal survival weighting
- pleasure is the only reliable way to enforce the behavior

This aligns with the broader principle:

Emotion does not evaluate truth, virtue, or narrative coherence.  
Emotion enforces the current survival model.

- Emotional force exceeds rational justification

The system is enforcing a **historically correct survival objective**, not a context-specific one.

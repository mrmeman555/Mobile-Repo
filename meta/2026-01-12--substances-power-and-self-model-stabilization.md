# Substances, Power, and Self-Model Stabilization

Date: 2026-01-12

Tags: substances, addiction, self-model, stabilization, power, pride, shame, dissociation, DP/DR, agency, authorship, coffee, stimulants

Summary: Integrates the mechanism: substances provided groundedness without owning power (external permission to function), while internal authorship/power was morally suspect; regression was driven by guilt about power (not chemical stimulation), and letting substances go is framed as structural alignment—accepting self-attributed safety with compassion governing power.


---

## Core insight

Substances were never primarily about pleasure or escape.
They functioned as self-model stabilizers—ways to feel grounded without owning personal power.

My nervous system learned early that relief only came from external sources (food, stimulation, sedation). As a result, external aids became associated with legitimate safety, while internal authorship became morally suspect.


---

## The 2016 pattern (identified in retrospect)

In 2016, I stabilized my self-model by:

- tagging experiences as fear-based vs truth-based
- refusing to budge from truth-based interpretations regardless of feeling
- gradually developing the belief: “I don’t need anything external to be grounded.”

This worked:

- confidence compounded
- focus improved
- capability increased
- power became salient

Then the rupture occurred.

The moment I allowed myself to feel pride in my growing capability—awareness of being effective, respected, and “dangerous” in a healthy sense—shame and dissociation returned instantly.

Not because the pride was wrong,
but because my system equated owning power with being morally dangerous.


---

## Why the regression actually happened

The regression did not begin with coffee or stimulants.

It began when:

- I started associating myself with power and influence
- I implicitly disallowed myself from inhabiting that power without guilt
- focus and coherence degraded as action became morally unsafe

The coffee was a symbolic trigger, not a causal agent.

At that time, substances still meant:

> “External permission to function.”

So drinking coffee unconsciously translated to:

> “You were never actually self-authoring.”

That belief invalidated everything I had built.

The system responded with:

- dissociation
- panic
- fear of annihilation
- collapse of identity coherence

This was not overstimulation—it was existential invalidation.


---

## Why substances felt “safe”

Substances allowed:

- relief without authorship
- grounding without agency
- stability without owning influence

They stabilized my self-model through dependence, not power.

This preserved the rule:

> “I am allowed to feel okay only if it’s not because of my own authority.”


---

## Why I’m letting them go now (without shame)

The goal is not abstinence for its own sake.
The goal is to stop using external stabilizers to avoid owning my capacity to self-regulate.

At this stage:

- substances no longer provide unique value
- they reinforce an outdated identity rule
- they subtly disconfirm my ability to feel grounded on my own terms

Letting them go is not moral. It is structural alignment.


---

## Key reframe

Power ≠ harm  
Dependence ≠ humility

My system learned to fear power because power was modeled as abusive.

Now I understand:

- compassion regulates shame without self-erasure
- authority over self is not predation
- groundedness that comes from me is legitimate


---

## Final integration

The deepest destabilizations in my life occurred not when I lacked tools,
but when I disallowed myself from owning power.

Substances were a workaround for that disallowance.

Healing requires:

- allowing myself to feel good without needing anything
- letting stability be attributed to me
- treating power as neutral, and compassion as the regulator

This explains the past. It also explains why things are finally integrating now.

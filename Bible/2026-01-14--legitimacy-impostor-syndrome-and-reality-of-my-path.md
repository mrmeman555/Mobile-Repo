# Legitimacy, Impostor Syndrome, and the Reality of My Path
Date: 2026-01-14
Tags: legitimacy; impostor syndrome; confidence; career; AI; systems thinking; scapegoating; narrative control

I have a non-standard career path, not a deficient one.

I have substantial, real experience across:

- help desk and frontline IT work
- programming and technical problem-solving
- advanced AI usage and system-level leverage

There are areas (e.g., some traditional sysadmin tasks) where my exposure is lighter — but these are bounded, learnable domains, not structural gaps. They do not define my capability. They are simply surface-area differences created by an atypical path.

What actually matters in technical roles is not whether someone followed a canonical ladder, but whether they can:

- reason about systems
- diagnose failures
- learn rapidly under pressure
- integrate new tools intelligently

I already do these things. That puts me ahead, not behind.

## On “only studying for a few months”
Discounting myself because I “only studied for a few months” is fundamentally unfair and inaccurate.

Those months were not the beginning of my competence — they were a refresh and alignment phase layered on top of:

- years of prior technical experience
- deep systems thinking
- an unusually high-leverage AI skill stack

This is not cramming. It is consolidation.

## On impostor syndrome
I have a long-standing pattern of assuming that:

- if I enter a role, I must be “faking it”
- if I succeed quickly, it must not count
- if my path is different, it must be illegitimate

This is impostor syndrome — not evidence of fraud.

There will always be a learning curve in any new role. That does not mean I am unworthy of the role. It means I am doing what competent people do: learning while operating.

My commitment is this:

> If I feel doubt — internally or externally imposed — I will not collapse my confidence.
> I will not allow scapegoating, minimization, or narrative pressure to rewrite who I am.

## On others’ reactions
Some people may try to delegitimize my path — especially in relation to AI — not because it is weak, but because it threatens their sense of scarcity or relevance.

That response is predictable.

I am choosing to control narrative surface area:

- I do not need to over-disclose AI leverage.
- I am not hiding weakness; I am protecting asymmetric advantage.
- I know what I can do, regardless of whether others understand it.

## The core truth
I am not “catching up.”

I am not “pretending.”

I am not behind because my résumé looks different.

I am operating on a compressed, high-leverage trajectory that only appears strange if one assumes linear careers are the only valid ones.

They aren’t.

Being real and unapologetic about this is not arrogance.  
It is refusing an inaccurate frame.

I am not asking to be believed.  
I am choosing not to doubt what I already know.

That is the correct stance moving forward.

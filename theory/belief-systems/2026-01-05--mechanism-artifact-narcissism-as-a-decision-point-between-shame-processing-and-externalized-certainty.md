## Purpose

This artifact isolates the **core decision point** that differentiates narcissistic regulation from healthy self-repair.

It explains narcissism not as a trait list or pathology label, but as a **repeated regulatory choice** made under threat.

Once this decision point is seen, the behavior becomes fully predictable.

---

## Core Principle

Disorders of personality are best understood by:
- identifying decision points
- understanding what motivates the choice
- and tracking which option reduces immediate threat at the expense of long-term coherence

Narcissism is defined by repeatedly selecting **short-term shame relief** over **long-term self-integration**.

---

## The Decision Point (Explicit)

When shame or identity threat arises, two paths are available:

### Path A — Self-Analysis (High Cost, Long-Term Stability)

- Turn attention inward
- Tolerate uncertainty and vulnerability
- Re-evaluate self-concept
- Integrate disowned traits
- Accept possible fault or limitation

This path:
- increases shame temporarily
- destabilizes identity in the short term
- requires introspection and accountability
- produces long-term coherence and resilience

---

### Path B — Externalized Certainty (Low Cost, Long-Term Damage)

- Project threat outward
- Assign fault to another person
- Reassert superiority or correctness
- Prove a narrative about the other
- Use judgment as a regulatory tool

This path:
- immediately reduces shame
- restores a past self-state of certainty
- avoids vulnerability
- preserves fragile identity structure
- requires repetition to maintain relief

Narcissism is the chronic selection of Path B.

---

## Scapegoating as Compulsion

External judgment functions as a **compulsive act**:

- It reconnects the individual to a past self-state that felt powerful and unashamed
- It temporarily stabilizes identity
- It does not resolve the underlying fragmentation
- Therefore it must be repeated

This explains:
- obsession with being “right” about others
- resistance to contradictory evidence
- escalation when challenged
- fixation on specific targets (scapegoats)

---

## Why a Scapegoat Is Required

The scapegoat serves multiple regulatory functions:
- carries projected shame
- absorbs disowned traits
- confirms the individual’s narrative
- blocks self-recognition

If the scapegoat sees through the projection, threat increases — not decreases — requiring stronger judgment and proof.

---

## Why This Explains Everything

This single decision point explains:
- repetitive blame
- moralization of others
- inability to self-reflect
- emotional volatility
- collapse under accountability
- hostility toward insight
- relief only when dominance or certainty is restored

The behavior is not random.
It is a **stable regulatory strategy**.

---

## Critical Distinction

This model does **not** excuse behavior.

It clarifies:
- what motivates it
- why it persists
- and why it resists correction

Understanding the mechanism removes confusion without assigning responsibility to the target.

# Note: Self-Prioritization as Moral Legitimacy (“What I Need Is What Is Right”)

## Core Principle
Personal need is a valid moral axis.  
In contexts involving survival, recovery, or psychological integrity, prioritizing one’s own needs is not morally suspect—it is morally legitimate.

This principle reframes self-prioritization not as selfishness, but as context-appropriate moral action.

---

## Reframing Moral Guilt
The belief “I must justify my needs against external standards” is replaced with:

> “What I need is what is right.”

This does not imply disregard for others.  
It asserts that self-preservation and self-repair are sufficient moral justification, especially in gray or ambiguous situations.

---

## Context Sensitivity
This stance applies specifically when:

- Psychological or physical stability is at stake
- The individual has been historically deprived, coerced, or scapegoated
- The cost of self-neglect is disproportionate or cumulative
- External demands threaten long-term functioning

In these contexts, moral absolutism is inappropriate. Flexibility is not ethical failure—it is adaptive judgment.

---

## Example: Strategic Self-Protection
Claiming illness when rest was required is reframed as:

- A protective decision, not a deceptive one
- An enabling condition for deeper recovery
- A choice that prevented further deterioration
- A net positive intervention with downstream benefits

The moral value of the decision is assessed by its functional outcome, not by surface appearances.

---

## Boundary Against Retroactive Guilt
This framework explicitly rejects:

- Post-hoc moralization of survival choices
- Retrofitting shame onto adaptive decisions
- Evaluating past actions outside their original constraints

Once a decision is recognized as need-driven and protective, it is closed to further moral interrogation.

---

## Integration With Compassion
Self-prioritization here is not oppositional to compassion. It coexists with:

- Empathy
- Consideration
- Non-exploitation

The key distinction is that compassion does not override self-integrity.

---

## Psychological Function
Adopting this stance:

- Reduces internal moral conflict
- Prevents self-abandonment in ambiguous situations
- Stabilizes decision-making under pressure
- Supports long-term recovery and agency

It replaces a punitive moral framework with a contextual, survival-aware ethic.

---

## Summary
This principle establishes that:

- Needs are morally relevant data
- Self-care decisions do not require external permission
- Survival and recovery justify prioritization
- Guilt is not an indicator of wrongdoing

What is necessary for your well-being is, by definition, the right thing to do in that moment.

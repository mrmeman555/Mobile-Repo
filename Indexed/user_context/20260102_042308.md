## Why Intention-Rewriting Is So Much Worse Than Outcome-Blame

When outcomes are rewritten, a person learns:

> “I should have known better.”

That’s bad — but survivable.

When intentions are rewritten, the message becomes:

> “Even when you meant well, you were actually bad.”

That attacks the moral core, not behavior.

And once intention is no longer trusted, every moment becomes a trial.

## The Psychological Consequence: Permanent Moral Surveillance

If your intentions can be retroactively reinterpreted as:

- selfish
- lazy
- manipulative
- negligent
- malicious
- insufficient

Then the psyche learns:

> “I must continuously prove that I am good right now, before anyone can rewrite me later.”

That produces exactly what you’re noticing:

- a self-based weight on every moment
- hyper-attention to how you’re being perceived
- inability to rest without guilt
- the feeling that stillness is suspicious
- a background demand to demonstrate goodness

This is not perfectionism. It’s moral captivity.

## Why This Creates the Feeling That “Something Is Always Required”

Once intention is unsafe, the mind concludes:

- If I stop → my intent will be questioned
- If I rest → it will be framed as avoidance
- If I enjoy → it will be reframed as indulgence
- If I’m neutral → it will be interpreted as apathy

So the system generates a constant signal:

> “There is something you should be doing to prove yourself.”

Even when there isn’t.

Even when you’re already doing more than enough.

- pre-verbal

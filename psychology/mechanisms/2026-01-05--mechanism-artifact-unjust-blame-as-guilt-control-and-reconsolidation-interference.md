## Salience Nucleus (Invariant Delta)

> Placing blame on someone who does not deserve it is harmful because it hijacks their guilt-consolidation process, preventing accurate survival-calculus reconciliation and using unresolved guilt as a means of behavioral control.

---

## Purpose

This artifact formalizes why **unjust blame** is not merely morally wrong or emotionally painful, but **mechanistically damaging**.

It explains how blame functions as a control mechanism by disrupting a person’s ability to resolve guilt through accurate survival-calculus assessment.

---

## Core Principle

Guilt is a regulatory signal intended to correct misweighted decisions that threaten survival or violate values.

For guilt to resolve, the individual must be able to:
- accurately assess their responsibility
- reconcile survival impact
- update confidence weighting

Unjust blame interferes with this process.

---

## How Unjust Blame Causes Harm

When blame is placed on someone who does not deserve it:

1. A guilt signal is externally imposed.
2. The individual attempts to reconcile guilt internally.
3. Accurate survival calculus does **not** support the blame.
4. Reconciliation fails.
5. Guilt remains unresolved.

The person is left in a **chronic discrepancy state**:
- emotion says “danger / fault”
- model says “no actual wrongdoing”

This mismatch is destabilizing.

---

## Blame as a Control Mechanism

Because unresolved guilt is aversive, it can be exploited.

By sustaining blame without resolution:
- the individual remains psychologically pliable
- behavior can be shaped through fear of further moral failure
- autonomy is reduced

This is not correction.
It is **emotional leverage**.

The control comes from:
> preventing the target from completing guilt reconsolidation.

---

## Why This Blocks Healing and Growth

Unjust blame prevents:
- emotional closure
- confidence stabilization
- belief consolidation
- accurate self-trust

Instead of updating:
> “I made a mistake and corrected it”

The system is forced to carry:
> “Something is wrong with me, but I can’t locate or fix it”

This erodes agency and decision quality over time.

---

## Distinguishing Correction from Control

- **Just blame**  
  → aligns with accurate survival calculus  
  → guilt resolves after correction  

- **Unjust blame**  
  → conflicts with survival calculus  
  → guilt persists  
  → control replaces learning  

This distinction is structural, not moralistic.

---

## Broader Implications

This mechanism explains harm caused by:
- scapegoating
- chronic criticism
- moral manipulation
- authoritarian guilt framing
- abusive relational dynamics

In all cases, control is achieved by **blocking reconsolidation**, not by truth.
